# Problem 9 – Change of reference frame (Copernican model → geocentric description)

Given:

$$
\vec r_Z(t)=R_Z(\cos(\omega_Z t),\sin(\omega_Z t))
$$

$$
\vec r_M(t)=R_M(\cos(\omega_M t),\sin(\omega_M t))
$$

---

## 1. Heliocentric system

In the heliocentric frame:

- Earth moves on a circle of radius \(R_Z\),
- Mars moves on a circle of radius \(R_M\).

Their coordinates are:

$$
x_Z(t)=R_Z\cos(\omega_Z t),\qquad y_Z(t)=R_Z\sin(\omega_Z t)
$$

$$
x_M(t)=R_M\cos(\omega_M t),\qquad y_M(t)=R_M\sin(\omega_M t)
$$

---

## 2. Position of Mars relative to Earth

We compute:

$$
\vec r_{M/Z}(t)=\vec r_M(t)-\vec r_Z(t)
$$

Thus:

$$
\vec r_{M/Z}(t)=
\big(R_M\cos(\omega_M t)-R_Z\cos(\omega_Z t),\;
R_M\sin(\omega_M t)-R_Z\sin(\omega_Z t)\big)
$$

So the components are:

$$
x_{M/Z}(t)=R_M\cos(\omega_M t)-R_Z\cos(\omega_Z t)
$$

$$
y_{M/Z}(t)=R_M\sin(\omega_M t)-R_Z\sin(\omega_Z t)
$$

---

## 3. Interpretation

In the heliocentric system both planets move on circles around the Sun.

In the geocentric system, Mars is described relative to Earth, so its trajectory can look much more complicated, including retrograde-like motion.

---

## Final results

Heliocentric motion of Earth:

$$
\vec r_Z(t)=R_Z(\cos(\omega_Z t),\sin(\omega_Z t))
$$

Heliocentric motion of Mars:

$$
\vec r_M(t)=R_M(\cos(\omega_M t),\sin(\omega_M t))
$$

Geocentric position of Mars relative to Earth:

$$
\vec r_{M/Z}(t)=\vec r_M(t)-\vec r_Z(t)
$$

Components:

$$
x_{M/Z}(t)=R_M\cos(\omega_M t)-R_Z\cos(\omega_Z t)
$$

$$
y_{M/Z}(t)=R_M\sin(\omega_M t)-R_Z\sin(\omega_Z t)
$$
